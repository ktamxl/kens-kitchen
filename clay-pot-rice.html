Ultimate Clay Pot Rice

煲仔飯
Serves3–4
Rice PrepSoak 30–60 min + 1 hr fridge
Cook Time~35 min active
DifficultyIntermediate
Base Quantity3 cups rice

Clay pot rice is one of those dishes where the vessel is the recipe. The thick clay walls absorb and radiate heat differently than any metal pot — slower, more even, more patient. The result is rice that is firm and separate on top, fragrant with sesame and soy throughout, and crowned at the bottom with 鍋巴 — the golden, crackling crust that every diner races to scrape. This is Ken's version: built around the right rice, the right pot, and a precise sequence of tilts and timers that turn a home stove into a clay pot specialist.

01

Ingredients

The Rice Base

台山丝苗米 (Taishan Simiao Rice)3 cups
Water2¾ cups
Sesame oil (to coat pot)~1 tbsp
Water ratio: Standard is 1:1 — but Ken removes ¼ cup for firmer, non-mushy rice. 3 cups rice → 2¾ cups water. Always.

Toppings

Chicken thigh, cut into bite-sized chunks400–500g
Lap Cheong (Chinese sausage), sliced diagonally2 links
Dried shiitake mushrooms, rehydrated & sliced6–8 pieces
Fresh ginger, thinly sliced6–8 slices

Chicken Marinade

Light soy sauce1½ tbsp
Oyster sauce1 tbsp
Sesame oil1 tsp
Cornstarch1 tsp
Sugar½ tsp
White pepperpinch
Shaoxing wine1 tbsp

For the Crust & Finish

Cooking oil (drizzled around lid edge)2–3 tbsp

02

Instructions

  1. 1
    Wash, Soak & Refrigerate the Rice
    Rinse the rice 3–4 times in cold water. Each time, gently scrub the grains between your palms in a praying motion 🙏 — you will feel the water go from milky-white to progressively clearer. On the final rinse, keep the water and let the rice soak for 30 minutes to 1 hour. Then strain off all the water and place the wet rice — uncovered — in the refrigerator for 1 hour. This drying step removes surface moisture so your water-to-rice ratio lands exactly right every time.
    Still damp after the fridge? Pat gently with a paper towel before adding to the pot.
  2. 2
    Marinate the Toppings
    Combine the chicken with all marinade ingredients. Add the sliced shiitake, lap cheong, and ginger to the same bowl and toss together until everything is well coated. Marinate for at least 30 minutes. The marinade juice is exactly what will seep down through the rice holes during cooking and flavour the grains from within.
  3. 3
    Oil the Clay Pot
    Pour about 1 tablespoon of sesame oil into the dry clay pot. Use a silicone brush to coat the entire interior — bottom and all the way up the sides. This is the foundation of your 鍋巴 crust and prevents rice from fusing to the clay.
  4. 4
    Add the Rice & Water
    Transfer the refrigerated rice into the oiled pot. Pour in 2¾ cups of cold water. Gently stir to level the rice evenly across the bottom.
  5. 5
    High Heat — Uncovered — Stay Close
    Place the clay pot on the stove over high heat. Do not cover it. Do not leave the station. Keep a chopstick within reach. You will watch the water go from still to steaming, then begin to boil.
  6. 6
    First Stir at the Boil
    The moment you see the water start to boil — bubbles forming at the edges and breaking through the rice surface — use a chopstick to give the rice a quick, gentle stir. Make sure no rice is sticking to the bottom. This is a brief but critical move: uneven contact with the hot bottom can cause patchy burning.
  7. 7
    Poke Holes & Add Toppings — Act Fast
    Keep watching. When the water is nearly gone from the surface — rice looks moist but no longer submerged — act quickly. Use the blunt end of a chopstick to poke holes all over the rice. This releases trapped steam from the bottom and opens channels for the topping marinade to seep down. Then immediately pour the marinated chicken, sausage, mushrooms, and ginger evenly over the rice surface.
  8. 8
    Cover, Lower Heat & Drizzle Oil Around the Lid
    Cover immediately. Turn heat down to low-medium (Ken uses level 4 on his stove — yours may vary; this is the one step that needs a test run or two to calibrate). Then drizzle cooking oil in a steady stream around the top edge of the lid, just as you did with the sesame oil. The oil seeps down the sides toward the bottom, where it fries the rice against the clay to build the 鍋巴. Set your timer: 6 minutes.
  9. 9
    The Shifting Sequence — Building the Crust
    This is where your wok ring earns its place. After the 6-minute base cook, use the wok ring to tilt and rotate the pot through four positions, allowing the gas flame to reach the sides of the clay pot and build an even crust all the way around. Use a multi-timer app to sequence the shifts with 1 minute between each.

  10. 10
    Return Flat, Turn Off Flame & Add Finishing Soy Sauce
    Return the pot to a flat position on the burner. Turn the stove off completely. Do NOT open the lid. Now drizzle Lee Kum Kee Sweet Soy Sauce around the top edge of the lid — exactly the same motion as the cooking oil earlier. The sauce seeps down the sides, glazing the rice with that characteristic sweet-savory flavour. Let the pot rest undisturbed on the stove for 8 minutes. The residual heat finishes cooking the chicken gently.
    Do not open the lid during the 8-minute rest. The steam inside is still cooking the chicken. Opening early lets it escape and leaves the top dry.
  11. 11
    Open, Season & Serve
    After 8 minutes, open the lid. The reveal should be dramatic — steam rising, glistening toppings, a perfume of soy and sesame. Add more sweet soy sauce directly over the toppings to taste. Stir everything together gently, mixing the sauce and rendered lap cheong fat into the rice. Serve immediately, and make sure everyone gets a share of the bottom crust.
    The bottom: Use a spoon to scrape the golden crust from the sides and bottom — that is the best bite in the pot.
03

Chef's Notes

🍳

The Rice Is Non-Negotiable

Taishan Simiao (台山亦草米) is the only rice Ken uses. Its slender grain tolerates the high heat and develops crust without going mushy. Regular jasmine rice will work in a pinch, but the texture will be softer.

⏱

The Fridge Step Is Real

Refrigerating the soaked rice sounds strange, but it removes that unpredictable layer of surface moisture. Your 2¾ cup water ratio becomes reliable and repeatable after this step.

🔥

Heat Level Is Stove-Specific

Ken uses level 4 for the covered cook. Your stove may run hotter or cooler. First run: open the lid at 6 minutes and check. If rice seems undercooked, add 2 min; if it smells like burning, drop to level 3 next time.

🫘

The Wok Ring

The ring is essential for the tilt sequence — it gives the rounded clay pot a stable cradle at an angle. Without it, the pot sits flat and you only crisp the very bottom, not the sides.

🆊

Lap Cheong Does Two Jobs

The sausage flavours the marinade, and its fat renders during cooking and seeps into the rice. It is not just a topping — it is a seasoning agent. Don't skip it.

🏥

Variations

The topping is flexible: try salted fish and pork belly (the classic), frog legs, or a simple egg cracked in at step 10 before covering. The rice technique stays exactly the same.

Scaling This Recipe

The base recipe uses 3 cups of rice and fits a standard-sized clay pot. To scale up, you need a larger pot — the rice layer must remain roughly the same depth for even cooking.

Base: 3 cups rice, 2¾ cups water, 400–500g toppings. Water ratio stays at 2¾ per 3 cups of rice.
